Employment Verification Letter

An employment verification letter confirms a person’s work relationship and may include job title, employment dates, status, compensation, or other facts authorized for release.

What is an employment verification letter?

An employment verification letter is a written confirmation of a person’s current or past employment. It is often issued by an employer, human resources department, payroll office, manager, or authorized company representative. The letter may state the employee’s name, job title, employment status, start date, work location, scheduled hours, and compensation when the employee has authorized that information to be released.

The letter should answer the recipient’s request without sharing unnecessary personnel information. Some organizations use a standard form or direct verification service instead of a letter. Before drafting anything, the employee and employer should identify who is authorized to sign and what information may legally and properly be disclosed.

When is it commonly used?

Employment verification letters are frequently used for rental applications, mortgage files, loans, immigration-related submissions, school enrollment, benefits, background screening, professional licensing, and travel or visa documentation. A recipient may ask for current employment, expected continued employment, or a history of prior work.

Requirements vary widely. A lender may request income and probability of continued employment, while a landlord may need only position and start date. A foreign agency may request notarization or company documents. Use the recipient’s checklist and avoid adding statements the employer cannot confirm.

Who typically needs it?

Employees and former employees request these letters, while employers or authorized representatives usually sign them. Independent contractors may need a different letter describing their contract relationship because they are not employees. Self-employed individuals may be asked for tax, business, banking, or accountant records instead.

The signer should have authority to speak for the employer and access to reliable records. A notary verifies the signer’s identity and notarial act, but does not confirm that the signer has corporate authority or that the employment information is accurate.

Is notarization legally required?

Most routine employment verification letters are not automatically required by law to be notarized. Many recipients accept a letter on company letterhead with an authorized signature and contact information. Notarization may be requested for international use, a sworn declaration, a high-risk transaction, or an agency-specific process.

If the recipient requires notarization, ask whether it needs an acknowledgment, jurat, witness signature, or another certificate. The employer should not sign in advance when the notary must witness the signature or administer an oath. Notarization does not replace company authorization, letterhead, or supporting payroll records.

Can it be notarized online?

An employment verification letter may be eligible for remote online notarization when the signer and receiving party can use an electronically signed document. The authorized representative joins a live video session, completes identity verification, and signs as instructed.

International authorities, lenders, or agencies may require a wet-ink original, a corporate seal, authentication, or a particular local process. Confirm those rules before using online notarization. If accepted, SafeNotarize can complete the eligible notarial act without requiring the company representative to travel to a notary office.

Identification and information to prepare

The authorized signer needs an acceptable unexpired government-issued photo ID and access to the final letter. The signer should use a name consistent with the ID and the signature block. The session also requires email, a camera, a microphone, and a reliable internet connection.

Prepare the employee’s name, position, dates of employment, current status, employer name, company contact details, and only the compensation information authorized for release. Include the recipient’s reference number when requested. Company letterhead or an official email domain may help the recipient verify the source, but the notary does not control those business requirements.

Step-by-step process

  1. Get the request in writing. Ask the recipient exactly which facts and format are required.
  2. Obtain employee authorization. Follow company privacy procedures before releasing salary or personnel data.
  3. Choose an authorized signer. Confirm who may issue employment statements for the company.
  4. Draft from reliable records. Use accurate dates, titles, status, and contact information.
  5. Confirm notarization instructions. Determine the required notarial act and whether remote notarization is accepted.
  6. Complete the online session. Upload the final letter and sign only when directed.
  7. Deliver securely. Send the notarized letter using the recipient’s requested method and retain an appropriate company copy.

What should be reviewed before signing?

Confirm spelling, employment dates, job title, salary frequency, and whether the employee is full-time, part-time, temporary, or no longer employed. Avoid predictions about future employment unless the company is authorized and able to make them. Include a business contact who can respond to verification requests.

Review confidentiality obligations and remove sensitive information that the recipient did not request. The letter should not include Social Security numbers, full banking details, or unrelated personnel information. Employers should follow their own policies and applicable privacy rules.

Frequently asked questions

Who should sign an employment verification letter?

An authorized employer representative should sign, such as a human resources employee, manager, owner, or payroll representative. Company policy determines who has that authority.

Does the employee sign the letter?

Usually the employer representative signs the verification letter. The employee may sign a separate authorization allowing the employer to release specified information.

Must salary be included?

Only when the recipient requires it and the employee has authorized its release. Many letters confirm employment without disclosing compensation.

Can a former employer issue the letter?

Yes, if company records are available and an authorized representative can accurately confirm the prior employment. The letter should clearly state that the employment has ended.

Can a contractor use an employment verification letter?

A contractor should generally use wording that accurately describes an independent contract relationship. Calling a contractor an employee can create confusion or misrepresentation.

Does online notarization verify the employer?

No. The notary verifies the signer’s identity and performs the notarial act. The recipient may separately verify the employer, signer’s authority, and employment facts.

Can the notarized letter be emailed?

Often yes, especially when the recipient accepts electronic documents. Ask whether it needs the electronic original, a printed copy, a wet-ink original, or direct delivery from the employer.
