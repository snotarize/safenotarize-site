Child Passport Consent Letter for Separate Consent Needs

This page covers a custom consent letter only when the receiving organization permits a separate letter. It is not automatically a replacement for the official DS-3053 form.

What is a child passport consent letter?

A child passport consent letter is a written statement showing that a parent or guardian gives permission for a passport-related action involving a minor. It may be used when one parent cannot appear, when a guardian is authorizing another responsible adult, or when an agency asks for additional written consent. The document normally identifies the child, the consenting adult, the person receiving permission, and the specific action being authorized.

When is this document commonly used?

A consent letter may be requested when a minor’s passport application cannot be completed with every parent or guardian present. It can also support a file when a parent is temporarily unavailable, lives in another location, or needs to authorize a trusted adult to help with a specific step. Some families prepare a letter to explain the limited permission being granted and to provide reliable contact information.

The U.S. Department of State and other passport authorities may direct applicants to an official consent form for certain situations. SafeNotarize already provides an internal guide to the official DS-3053 passport consent form. Always follow the current instructions of the agency processing the application.

Official DS-3053 or a separate consent letter?

Need the official DS-3053 form? Use the current U.S. Department of State form when the passport authority instructs you to complete it.

Looking for a separate child passport consent letter? Continue with this page only when the receiving organization allows a custom letter for a different consent need. A custom letter is not automatically a substitute for DS-3053.

Who typically needs it?

Parents, legal guardians, custodians, and adults helping a child with a passport application are the most common users. A family member, travel coordinator, or authorized representative may also need a copy when assisting with document delivery or an appointment. The person signing should have the legal authority to give the permission described in the letter.

Is notarization legally required?

Notarization requirements depend on the exact passport process and the document being signed. A general consent letter may be accepted without notarization by one recipient and rejected by another. An official form may contain its own notarial certificate and signature instructions. Follow the form and agency instructions rather than assuming that every consent letter has the same rule.

When notarization is requested, the notary verifies the signer’s identity, completes the appropriate notarial act, and records the session as required. The notary does not verify custody rights, approve the passport application, or guarantee that an agency will accept the document.

Can a child passport consent letter be notarized online?

Many consent documents can be handled through remote online notarization when the signer, the notary, the document, and the receiving organization meet the applicable requirements. The signer appears by live audio-video connection, presents identification, and signs electronically when instructed. Acceptance can vary, especially for official passport forms or international use.

Before booking, ask the receiving agency whether it accepts a remotely notarized document and whether an original wet-ink document is required. If online notarization is acceptable, SafeNotarize can help the signer complete a live session from a phone, tablet, or computer.

Identification and information to prepare

An adult signer should have an unexpired government-issued photo ID that is accepted by the online notary platform. The name on the ID should reasonably match the name used in the document. The signer may also need a device with a working camera, microphone, internet connection, and access to email.

Prepare the child’s full legal name, date and place of birth, the consenting adult’s contact information, the name of the authorized adult, and a clear description of the passport-related permission. Include dates or limits when the permission is temporary. Keep supporting records available in case the receiving agency requests them, but do not upload unnecessary sensitive records to a document unless they are required.

Step-by-step process

  1. Confirm the requirement. Ask the passport agency which letter, form, and supporting records are required.
  2. Prepare the document. Use accurate names, dates, relationships, and a specific statement of consent.
  3. Review before signing. Check spelling and confirm that the permission is not broader than intended.
  4. Leave required signatures blank. Do not sign early if the notary must witness or acknowledge the signature.
  5. Book the session. Upload the final document through the existing SafeNotarize process and join with acceptable identification.
  6. Submit it correctly. Follow the receiving agency’s instructions for originals, copies, attachments, and delivery.

What should be reviewed before signing?

Check that the document identifies the correct child and adults, states the exact passport action being authorized, and includes reliable contact details. Remove vague language that could be misunderstood. Confirm whether the agency wants a separate consent form for each child and whether the letter must be recent.

When is notarization commonly requested?

A passport office, embassy, consulate, court, or another receiving authority may request a notarized signature. A custom letter is not automatically a substitute for DS-3053 or another official form; confirm the exact document before signing.

Common mistakes to avoid

  • Using a custom letter when the official DS-3053 form was requested.
  • Using incomplete parent, child, or passport-application information.
  • Signing before the required notarial session.
  • Assuming notarization guarantees passport acceptance.

Frequently asked questions

Is a general consent letter the same as Form DS-3053?

No. A general letter and an official passport form serve different purposes. If the passport agency requires Form DS-3053 or another official form, use that form and follow its current instructions.

Can one parent sign for the other parent?

A person may sign only when legally authorized to do so. A notary cannot determine parental authority or allow someone to sign another person’s name without a valid legal basis.

Should I sign the letter before the online session?

Usually, leave the signature blank when the notary must witness the signing or take an acknowledgment. Follow the receiving agency’s instructions and sign only when the notary directs you.

Does the child need to join the notary session?

The adult whose signature is being notarized normally must appear. Whether the child must be present depends on the passport process and the receiving agency, not on the notarization alone.

Can an online notarized consent letter be used outside the United States?

Possibly, but acceptance varies by country, agency, and document type. Confirm whether remote notarization, an original document, authentication, or another legalization step is required.

What happens if the name on the ID is different?

Minor differences may require additional review. Use the signer’s current legal name, bring supporting documentation when appropriate, and contact SafeNotarize before the session if the difference is significant.

Does notarization guarantee passport approval?

No. Notarization verifies the signing process and identity for the notarial act. The passport agency independently decides whether the application and supporting documents meet its requirements.
