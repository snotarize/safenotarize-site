Business Authorization Letter

A business authorization letter allows an employee, manager, agent, contractor, or representative to perform defined tasks for a business.

What is a business authorization letter?

A business authorization letter is a written statement from an authorized owner, officer, manager, or other business signer giving a named person permission to complete specific tasks. The letter may authorize an employee, agent, contractor, accountant, courier, property manager, or representative to act within a limited scope and time.

The document should clearly identify what the representative may and may not do. It does not automatically create unlimited agency authority and may not replace a corporate resolution, power of attorney, board approval, bank form, government form, or contract required for a particular transaction.

Common situations in which it is used

Businesses may use authorization letters for document pickup, mail collection, account inquiries, vendor communication, permit applications, government filings, vehicle or property access, administrative tasks, record requests, contract delivery, or representation at a meeting. A bank may request its own account authority form. A government office may require evidence of registration, ownership, or officer status.

The letter should match the actual assignment. General language such as “handle all matters” can create confusion or be rejected when the recipient expects a narrow authorization.

Who may need it?

Sole proprietors, companies, partnerships, nonprofit organizations, property owners, employers, and professional offices may prepare the letter. The person signing should have authority under the business’s governing documents or role. The authorized representative and receiving organization may each keep a copy for their records.

Information commonly included

Business and signer information

Include the business’s legal name, trade name when relevant, address, registration or account number when requested, and contact information. Identify the signer’s full name, title, and basis of authority. The recipient may request articles, a business license, an operating agreement, a corporate record, or another document supporting the signer’s role.

Representative, scope, and limits

List the authorized person’s legal name, contact information, job or relationship, and identification details only when required. Describe the exact actions allowed, the account, property, documents, agency, or transaction involved, the effective and expiration dates, and any dollar, location, or decision limits. State whether delegation is prohibited.

Authorization letter compared with a resolution or power of attorney

An authorization letter is often used for a straightforward, limited task. A corporate resolution records a formal decision by directors, members, or another governing body. A power of attorney may grant broader legal authority and can have special execution rules. The receiving institution decides which document is acceptable. Do not use a simple letter when a required resolution, bank form, or power of attorney applies.

Is notarization required?

Many routine business authorization letters are accepted with an ordinary signature, but a bank, government office, property holder, contract party, or other recipient may require notarization. Requirements vary by institution, jurisdiction, and transaction. A notary verifies the signer and notarial act. The notary does not certify that the business exists or that the signer has corporate authority unless the law and certificate specifically provide otherwise.

Can a business authorization letter be notarized online?

Remote online notarization may be available if the signer, document, notary, and recipient meet applicable requirements. The business signer appears by live audio-video connection, completes identity verification, and signs or acknowledges the letter as directed. Confirm whether the receiving institution accepts electronic signatures and remote notarization and whether it requires supporting business records.

Identification commonly needed

The signer generally needs an acceptable, unexpired government-issued photo ID. The recipient may separately ask for a business license, formation record, letterhead, officer list, resolution, employment record, or account document. The authorized representative may need to show their own ID when using the letter, even if their signature is not notarized.

Important acceptance considerations

A recipient may refuse a broad, expired, unclear, or unsupported authorization. Banks, government agencies, title companies, courts, and contract parties often use mandatory forms. The letter should not authorize regulated acts that the representative is not licensed or legally permitted to perform. Review the authorization when employment or ownership changes. Common mistakes to avoid

  • Failing to identify the business and signer’s position.
  • Giving authority that is broader than the intended task.
  • Omitting start, end, revocation, or transaction limits.
  • Ignoring a recipient’s corporate-resolution or power-of-attorney requirement.

Frequently asked questions

Can an employee be authorized to pick up business documents?

Yes, a letter may identify the employee and the specific records or items, but the holder may require its own form and identification.

Can a business authorization letter allow someone to use a bank account?

A bank may require account-specific forms, resolutions, signature cards, or a power of attorney. Confirm the bank’s requirements before preparing a letter.

Who should sign the authorization letter?

An owner, officer, manager, or other person with sufficient business authority should sign. The recipient may request evidence of that role.

Should the letter have an expiration date?

A clear expiration date or completion event helps limit authority and may improve acceptance. Some recipients require a recently dated letter.

Is a business authorization letter the same as a power of attorney?

No. A letter often grants narrow task-based permission, while a power of attorney may create broader legal authority and special execution requirements.

Can the letter be notarized if the representative is in another state?

Usually the business signer is the person who must appear for notarization. The representative’s location matters only if their signature or participation is also required.

Does notarization prove that the signer has authority for the business?

Not automatically. Notarization verifies identity and the notarial act. The recipient evaluates business records and decides whether the signer has sufficient authority.
