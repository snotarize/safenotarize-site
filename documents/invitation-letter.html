Invitation Letter

An invitation letter explains who is being invited, the purpose and dates of the visit or event, and the host’s relationship and arrangements.

Prepare before the notary session
  1. Confirm whether the recipient requires a specific invitation format or supporting documents.
  2. Use complete host and visitor names exactly as shown on identification or travel records.
  3. State the purpose, dates, accommodation, expenses, and responsibilities clearly.
  4. Do not promise approval or claim that notarization guarantees acceptance.

What is an invitation letter?

An invitation letter is a written message from a person, family, organization, or business asking another person to visit, attend an event, participate in a meeting, or stay for a defined period. It can explain the relationship between the host and visitor, the reason for the invitation, expected dates, accommodation plans, and who will pay particular expenses.

The letter may be informal or may support a formal application. Its value depends on the receiving institution’s requirements and the accuracy of the information. A notarized invitation letter does not create a right to travel, enter a country, receive a visa, or attend an event.

Common situations in which it is used

Families may write invitation letters for relatives or friends visiting for holidays, graduations, weddings, births, medical support, or other personal events. Businesses may invite a customer, vendor, contractor, speaker, employee, or partner to attend meetings, training, conferences, site visits, or negotiations. Community and religious organizations may use similar letters for programs and ceremonies.

Travel-related letters are often reviewed with other evidence. The host should identify the real purpose of the visit and avoid statements that cannot be supported.

Who may need it?

A host, sponsor, family member, event organizer, employer, or authorized business representative may prepare the letter. The visitor may need it as part of a visa, travel, lodging, event, or administrative file. The person signing should have personal knowledge of the invitation and authority to make any stated commitments.

Information commonly included

Host and visitor details

Common details include each person’s full legal name, date of birth when relevant, address, phone number, email, passport or identification information when requested, nationality, and relationship. A business letter may include the organization’s legal name, address, contact person, and the signer’s position.

Visit and responsibility details

The letter may state arrival and departure dates, destination, event or meeting purpose, accommodation address, planned activities, and who will pay travel, lodging, food, medical, or other expenses. Financial commitments should be realistic and consistent with any supporting records. Include only information the receiving authority requests.

Invitation letter compared with an affidavit of support

An invitation letter focuses on the reason for the visit and the host’s arrangements. An affidavit of support is a sworn statement about financial support and may be requested under a separate process. A financial support letter may also describe expenses without serving the same purpose as an official government sponsorship form. Use the document named by the recipient.

Is notarization required?

Many invitation letters do not legally require notarization. A consulate, embassy, school, event organizer, business, or other recipient may nevertheless request a notarized signature. Requirements vary by country, jurisdiction, institution, and purpose. Notarization verifies the signer and notarial act; it does not verify every travel statement or guarantee a visa or government decision.

Can an invitation letter be notarized online?

Remote online notarization may be available if the signer, document, notary, and recipient meet applicable rules. The host appears by live audio-video connection, completes identity verification, and signs or acknowledges the letter as directed. For international use, confirm whether the destination accepts remote notarization or requires an original, apostille, authentication, or consular step.

Identification commonly needed

The signer generally needs an acceptable, unexpired government-issued photo ID. A business signer may also need records showing their position or authority, although the notary does not independently certify corporate authority unless a specific notarial act requires it. The signer needs a camera-enabled device, microphone, reliable internet, and email access.

Important acceptance considerations

An invitation letter is supporting information, not an approval document. Government agencies may evaluate employment, finances, travel history, family ties, security, and other evidence independently. Businesses and event organizers may also require official registration or contracts. Do not alter facts to strengthen an application. Common mistakes to avoid

  • Leaving the visitor, host, address, dates, or purpose incomplete.
  • Promising financial support without explaining the amount or evidence.
  • Using an invitation letter when the recipient requires an affidavit or official form.
  • Assuming the letter or notarization guarantees a visa or admission decision.

Frequently asked questions

Does an invitation letter guarantee visa approval?

No. A visa or immigration authority makes its own decision. The letter is only one possible supporting document and notarization does not guarantee approval.

Can I invite a friend or family member to stay at my home?

Yes, you may describe the invitation and accommodation, but the receiving authority decides what evidence and wording it requires.

Who should pay the visitor’s expenses?

The letter should truthfully state who expects to pay each expense. The visitor, host, employer, or another sponsor may be responsible depending on the arrangement.

Is an invitation letter the same as an affidavit of support?

No. An invitation letter explains the visit, while an affidavit of support focuses on financial support and may involve separate requirements.

Should passport numbers be included?

Include passport or identification numbers only when the recipient requests them. Protect sensitive information and verify every number before submission.

Can a business invitation letter be notarized online?

It may be, if remote notarization is permitted and the recipient accepts it. The signer should also have any requested evidence of business authority.

Can the letter be used internationally after online notarization?

Possibly, but international acceptance varies. Ask whether the destination requires a wet-ink original, apostille, authentication, translation, or consular processing.
