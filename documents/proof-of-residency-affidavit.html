Proof of Residency Affidavit

A proof of residency affidavit is a sworn statement used to confirm where a person lives when an organization requests written address verification.

Prepare before the notary session
  1. Ask the receiving organization for its exact affidavit or address-verification requirements.
  2. Use the resident’s full legal name and complete current address.
  3. Gather requested support such as a lease, utility bill, ID, bank statement, or official mail.

What is a proof of residency affidavit?

A proof of residency affidavit is a written statement in which a person swears or affirms that an individual lives at a stated address. The signer may be the resident, a landlord, a homeowner, a leaseholder, or a family member who has personal knowledge of the living arrangement. The document often explains when the resident moved in and the signer’s relationship to the address.

An affidavit is different from ordinary mail or a utility bill because it contains a formal statement and may include a notarial certificate. The receiving organization determines whether an affidavit is acceptable and what supporting evidence must accompany it.

Common situations in which it is used

Schools may request residency proof for enrollment or district eligibility. Licensing agencies, benefit programs, banks, employers, courts, immigration-related processes, insurers, and other administrative offices may also ask for address verification. A person who lives with relatives, has no utility account in their name, recently moved, or uses a shared lease may need an affidavit when standard documents are unavailable.

The purpose should be stated accurately. A school affidavit may require different facts from a bank or licensing affidavit, and an organization may reject a general statement when its own form is mandatory.

Who may sign the affidavit?

Depending on the recipient, the signer may be the resident making a self-sworn statement or another person confirming the residence. Landlords, homeowners, primary tenants, spouses, parents, and adult family members are common third-party signers. The signer should have personal knowledge and should not state facts they cannot truthfully confirm.

Information and supporting evidence commonly included

Affidavit details

The document commonly lists the resident’s legal name, complete address, move-in date, length of residence, and the purpose of the affidavit. A third-party signer may include their own address, relationship to the resident, ownership or lease status, and an explanation of how they know the resident lives there.

Supporting documents

The recipient may request a lease, deed, utility bill, driver license, state ID, bank statement, insurance record, tax document, school record, benefits letter, or official mail. Supporting records do not replace the affidavit unless the receiving organization says they do. Submit only current documents and protect unnecessary account numbers or sensitive information.

Is notarization required?

Some recipients require the affidavit to be sworn or acknowledged before a notary; others accept an unsigned declaration or a signed letter. The rule depends on the agency, jurisdiction, institution, and purpose. Read the requested form carefully. A notary verifies identity and administers the required notarial act but does not investigate whether the address is true or approve eligibility.

Can a proof of residency affidavit be notarized online?

Remote online notarization may be available when the document and receiving organization permit it. The signer appears by live audio-video connection, completes identity verification, and signs or acknowledges the affidavit as directed. Before booking, ask whether the recipient accepts electronic signatures, electronic notarial certificates, and remotely notarized documents.

Identification commonly needed

The signer generally needs an acceptable, unexpired government-issued photo ID. The ID may show a different address, particularly after a recent move, but the recipient may separately require evidence of the current residence. The signer also needs a camera-enabled device, microphone, reliable internet, and email access for the online session.

Important acceptance considerations

A notarized affidavit is not a substitute for every proof-of-address requirement. Some agencies require two independent records, documents issued within a certain period, or a statement signed by a property owner. Others use their own school, licensing, immigration, or benefits form. Confirm the exact requirements before paying for notarization. SafeNotarize does not determine residency eligibility and does not provide legal advice.

Frequently asked questions

Can I sign my own proof of residency affidavit?

Possibly. Some organizations accept a self-sworn affidavit, while others require a landlord, homeowner, leaseholder, or family member to confirm the address.

What if no utility bill is in my name?

The recipient may accept a lease, official mail, bank statement, insurance record, or a third-party affidavit. Ask for its exact list of acceptable evidence.

Does the address on my ID have to match?

Not always, especially after a recent move, but the organization may require additional current address evidence. The notary uses the ID primarily to verify identity.

Can a landlord notarize the affidavit without the resident present?

The signer whose signature is being notarized must appear. Whether the resident must also participate depends on whose signature the document requires.

Should I use a letter or an affidavit?

Use the format requested by the receiving organization. An affidavit is generally sworn or affirmed, while a letter may be a simpler signed statement.

Can a remotely notarized affidavit be used for school enrollment?

It may be accepted, but school districts set their own residency rules. Confirm that the district accepts remote online notarization and electronic documents.

Does notarization prove that the address is true?

No. The notary verifies the signer and performs the notarial act. The recipient evaluates the statement and evidence and decides whether residency is established.
