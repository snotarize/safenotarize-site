Letter of Authorization Guide

A letter of authorization gives another person permission to perform a clearly limited task on the signer’s behalf, such as pickup, access, inquiry, or an administrative action.

What is a letter of authorization?

A letter of authorization is a written permission allowing another person to complete a specific task for the signer. It is commonly used when the signer cannot appear personally or wants a trusted representative to handle a limited administrative matter. The document should identify the representative and describe the authority narrowly enough that the recipient can understand what is permitted.

A letter of authorization is not automatically a power of attorney. It generally covers a defined task, location, account, item, or time period. If the matter involves broad legal authority, continuing financial control, real estate, healthcare decisions, or another regulated power, a formal power of attorney or organization-specific document may be required.

Common situations where it is used

People use authorization letters for document or package pickup, account inquiries, property access, vehicle matters, school records, utility issues, administrative filings, delivery acceptance, employment tasks, and communication with a business or agency. A business may authorize an employee or contractor to collect records or complete a specific filing.

The recipient may require a copy of the signer’s ID, the representative’s ID, an account number, title, registration, receipt, corporate record, or original signature. Ask for the exact checklist before creating the letter.

Who typically uses it?

The authorizing person should have the right to control the task, property, record, or account. The authorized representative may be a family member, employee, manager, agent, contractor, caregiver, or trusted friend. The receiving organization decides whether that representative and the letter are acceptable. Business signers should identify their title and authority to act for the company.

Information commonly included

Parties and authorized task

Include the full legal names and contact information of the authorizing person and representative. Describe the exact action: what may be picked up, accessed, submitted, discussed, released, signed, or received. Include relevant account, property, vehicle, case, order, or reference information without exposing unnecessary sensitive data.

Scope, dates, conditions, and identification

State the effective and expiration dates, location, frequency, dollar or transaction limits, prohibited actions, and whether the representative may delegate authority. Explain what identification or supporting records the representative will present. A precise letter helps prevent the representative from appearing to have broader authority than intended.

Authorization letter compared with power of attorney

A limited authorization letter usually addresses one task or short-term purpose. A power of attorney may appoint an agent with broader authority and may be governed by specific state law, witness, and notarization requirements. A recipient may reject a letter when a power of attorney, corporate resolution, court order, or proprietary form is required. Review the Power of Attorney notary page for general information.

Is notarization required?

Not every authorization letter must be notarized. A tow yard, bank, school, government office, property manager, carrier, dealer, employer, or other recipient may request notarization as an identity safeguard. Others accept a signed letter with copies of identification or require signing before an employee.

Confirm whether the recipient wants an acknowledgment, jurat, witness, original ink signature, or electronic document. Notarization does not expand the representative’s authority or guarantee that the task will be allowed.

Can it be notarized online?

Remote online notarization may be available when the signer and document qualify and the recipient accepts the electronic notarization. The signer verifies identity, appears by live audio-video connection, and signs electronically during the appointment.

Ask the organization whether it accepts an electronically signed authorization. Vehicle facilities, banks, foreign offices, and government agencies may require a paper original, proprietary form, or in-person process.

Identification commonly needed

The signer generally needs an acceptable, unexpired government-issued photo ID. The representative may also need their own photo ID when using the document. Depending on the task, the recipient may request account evidence, title, registration, receipt, business records, employment identification, or proof that the signer controls the property or account.

Important acceptance limitations

A recipient may require its own form, an original signature, a corporate resolution, a power of attorney, or direct verification from the signer. The letter may be rejected if it is too broad, expired, missing identification, or inconsistent with account or property records. Frequently asked questions

What tasks can a letter of authorization cover?

It can cover a specific permitted task such as pickup, access, inquiry, submission, receipt, or another limited administrative action accepted by the recipient.

Can the authorized person sign contracts for me?

Only if the document and applicable rules clearly permit it and the recipient accepts that authority. Many contracts require a formal power of attorney or direct signature.

Should I include an expiration date?

Yes. A clear end date or event helps limit the authority and may be required by the receiving organization.

Does the representative need identification?

Usually the recipient will require the representative’s government-issued photo ID and may also request a copy of the signer’s ID or supporting records.

Is a letter of authorization the same as a power of attorney?

No. A letter usually grants narrow task-specific permission, while a power of attorney may grant broader legal authority under specific execution rules.

Does the letter always need notarization?

No. Requirements depend on the recipient, task, jurisdiction, and supporting records. Ask before signing.

Can I notarize the letter online?

It may be possible when remote online notarization is permitted and the receiving organization accepts the electronic document and seal.
