No-sale vehicle transfer guide

Vehicle Gift Affidavit

A vehicle gift affidavit records that a donor is transferring a vehicle to a recipient without receiving a normal purchase price.

Prepare before the notary session
  1. Ask the motor vehicle agency which state forms, title documents, and tax records are required.
  2. Copy the VIN, year, make, model, title number, and odometer information accurately.
  3. Identify the donor, recipient, relationship, and whether any money or other value was exchanged.

What is a vehicle gift affidavit?

A vehicle gift affidavit is a sworn or signed statement explaining that ownership of a car, truck, motorcycle, trailer, or other vehicle is being transferred as a gift rather than through an ordinary sale. It commonly identifies the donor, recipient, vehicle, transfer date, relationship between the parties, and whether the donor received money or other consideration.

The affidavit may support a title and registration application, but it usually does not replace the signed title, odometer disclosure, tax form, lien release, insurance proof, or state motor vehicle form. The receiving agency determines the complete transfer package.

Common situations in which it is used

Parents may give a vehicle to an adult child, spouses may transfer a vehicle between themselves, and relatives may transfer a vehicle after a family decision. A business owner may also transfer a vehicle to or from a company, although tax and authority questions may be more complex. Some states use gift affidavits to determine whether sales or use tax applies or whether a family-transfer exemption is available.

A vehicle should not be described as a gift when money, debt cancellation, services, property, or another benefit was exchanged unless the form specifically allows and explains it.

Who may need it?

The donor, recipient, motor vehicle agency, tax office, insurer, lender, dealership, or title service may need the affidavit. One or both parties may be required to sign. If the vehicle is jointly owned, all required owners may need to participate. A lienholder’s written release or approval may also be necessary.

Information commonly included

People and transfer details

The document commonly lists the donor and recipient’s legal names, addresses, identification details when requested, relationship, transfer date, and a statement that the vehicle is a gift. It may ask whether any payment, debt, trade, or other value was exchanged and why the transfer is being made.

Vehicle details

Include the vehicle identification number, year, make, model, body type, color, title number, license plate, and current odometer reading when required. Copy the VIN carefully from the title or vehicle. A single incorrect digit can delay the transfer. State forms may require a separate odometer disclosure or damage statement.

Title, tax, registration, and insurance considerations

A gift does not automatically remove title fees, registration fees, taxes, insurance obligations, emissions requirements, or inspection rules. Tax treatment and family exemptions vary by state. The donor should remove or transfer plates only as directed, and the recipient should arrange insurance before driving. If the title is lost, branded, jointly owned, or subject to a lien, additional documents may be required.

Is notarization required?

Some states require notarization of a gift affidavit, title assignment, or related tax form, while others do not. The requirement depends on the state, form, vehicle type, and transaction. Follow the current motor vehicle agency instructions. A notary verifies the signer and notarial act but does not confirm ownership, tax eligibility, lien status, or the accuracy of the vehicle record.

Can a vehicle gift affidavit be notarized online?

Remote online notarization may be available when state law, the document, and the receiving motor vehicle agency allow it. The signer appears by live audio-video connection and completes identity verification. Before booking, confirm that the agency accepts an electronic signature and remote notarial certificate. Some title documents or state forms may still require wet-ink originals.

Identification commonly needed

Each signer whose signature is notarized generally needs an acceptable, unexpired government-issued photo ID. The parties should also have the current title, registration, VIN, odometer reading, lien information, and agency forms available. For online notarization, each participating signer needs the required device, internet connection, camera, microphone, and email access.

Motor vehicle forms are state-specific and may change. A general affidavit is not a substitute when the agency requires an official gift form. Common mistakes to avoid

  • Using a gift affidavit when money or other value was exchanged.
  • Entering VIN, title, mileage, owner, or recipient details incorrectly.
  • Failing to disclose a lien or confirm title eligibility.
  • Assuming the affidavit completes tax, insurance, title, or registration requirements.

Frequently asked questions

Is a vehicle gift affidavit the same as a bill of sale?

No. A bill of sale records a sale and purchase price, while a gift affidavit states that the vehicle is transferred without a normal sale price.

Can I give a vehicle to a non-relative?

Possibly, but tax exemptions and required forms may differ from family transfers. Check the motor vehicle and tax agency rules for the recipient’s state.

Does gifting a vehicle eliminate taxes and fees?

Not necessarily. Title, registration, use tax, gift tax, inspection, and other rules vary. The appropriate agency decides what is owed.

Must both donor and recipient sign?

The required signatures depend on the title, state form, ownership, and agency instructions. Confirm who must sign and whether each signature requires notarization.

Can I gift a vehicle that still has a loan?

A lienholder may control or restrict the transfer. Obtain the lender’s instructions and any required lien release or written approval before proceeding.

Can the title itself be notarized online?

It depends on the state and title form. Some agencies accept remote notarization, while others require an original wet-ink title and certificate.

Does notarization prove that the vehicle is free of liens?

No. Notarization verifies the signer and notarial act. A title search, lender record, or agency review determines lien and ownership status.
