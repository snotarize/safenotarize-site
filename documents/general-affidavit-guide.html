General Affidavit Guide

A general affidavit is a written statement a signer declares true under oath or affirmation. This guide explains common uses and notarization choices.

Prepare before creating or signing
  1. Confirm whether the receiving party requires its own affidavit form, wording, or notarial certificate.
  2. Use the affiant’s full legal name and write only facts the affiant can truthfully state from personal knowledge.
  3. Identify and label any supporting exhibits clearly before the signing appointment.
  4. Leave the signature and any jurat or acknowledgment section unsigned until the notary gives instructions.

What is a general affidavit?

A general affidavit is a written statement in which a person, called the affiant, presents facts and formally swears or affirms that the statements are true. Unlike an ordinary letter, an affidavit is designed for a formal use and normally includes a signature section and a notarial certificate. The document may be used when a court, agency, business, school, insurer, landlord, or other recipient wants a signed statement with added verification of the signing process.

Common situations where it is used

People use general affidavits to explain events, confirm relationships, document property or financial facts, describe lost records, support administrative requests, or provide a written account when no specialized form is available. A recipient may request an affidavit as part of a school, housing, insurance, business, immigration, licensing, or court-related process.

Who typically uses an affidavit?

An individual with personal knowledge of the facts usually signs as the affiant. The affiant may be a tenant, homeowner, family member, employee, business owner, witness, caregiver, customer, or other person who can truthfully explain the matter. Organizations may also ask an authorized representative to sign on behalf of a business, but the representative should confirm that the organization and recipient allow that authority.

Information commonly included

Affiant and factual statement

Most affidavits include the affiant’s full name, address or contact information when relevant, a clear title, and numbered or separated factual statements. Dates, places, names, account references, property descriptions, and other identifiers should be specific enough for the recipient to understand the statement. Avoid guesses, conclusions, or facts outside the affiant’s personal knowledge unless the document clearly identifies the source.

Exhibits, signature, and notary certificate

Supporting exhibits may include copies of letters, bills, photographs, records, receipts, or identification documents. Each exhibit should be labeled and referenced in the affidavit. The signature area should identify the affiant, and the notarial certificate should match the act requested by the recipient. A jurat generally involves an oath or affirmation and signing in the notary’s presence. An acknowledgment generally confirms that the signer signed voluntarily.

Affidavit compared with an ordinary letter

An ordinary letter communicates information but usually does not contain sworn language or a notarial certificate. An affidavit is more formal because the signer declares the facts under oath or affirmation when a jurat is used. That formality may be important to the recipient, but it also means the signer should read every statement carefully and correct any error before signing.

Is notarization required?

Not every written statement must be notarized. The need for notarization depends on the receiving party, jurisdiction, filing, transaction, and purpose. Some recipients require a jurat, others require an acknowledgment, and some accept a signed but unnotarized statement. Confirm the exact requirement before the session because a notary should not choose the certificate for the signer.

The receiving party may also require specific sworn wording, witness signatures, attachments, or a particular form. Notarization verifies the signing process; it does not replace those content requirements or guarantee acceptance.

Can a general affidavit be notarized online?

Remote online notarization may be available when the document, signer, notary, and receiving party meet applicable requirements. During an online session, the signer appears by live audio-video connection, completes identity verification, and signs electronically when directed. The notary completes the electronic certificate and applies the electronic seal.

Identification commonly needed

The affiant generally needs an acceptable, unexpired government-issued photo ID. The name on the ID should reasonably match the name shown in the affidavit. The signer also needs email access, reliable internet, and a device with a camera and microphone. Additional identity questions or credential analysis may be part of the online process.

Important acceptance limitations

A general affidavit may not be suitable when a statute, court, agency, lender, or institution requires a specialized form. The recipient may reject a document that lacks required language, supporting evidence, filing information, or the correct notarial certificate. SafeNotarize provides a general document tool and online notarization service, not legal advice. Users should confirm all content and acceptance requirements before relying on the document.

Common mistakes to avoid

  • Including opinions or facts the affiant does not personally know.
  • Leaving dates, names, events, or numbered facts unclear.
  • Failing to identify or attach referenced exhibits.
  • Signing before the jurat or acknowledgment session when the recipient requires live signing.

Frequently asked questions

What is the difference between an affidavit and a regular letter?

An affidavit is a formal statement intended to be sworn or affirmed and often notarized. A regular letter usually communicates information without sworn language or a notarial certificate.

Who is the affiant?

The affiant is the person who makes the factual statements and signs the affidavit. The affiant should have personal knowledge of the facts being stated.

Does a general affidavit need a jurat or an acknowledgment?

The receiving party should specify the required notarial act. A jurat generally includes an oath or affirmation, while an acknowledgment confirms voluntary signing.

Can I attach documents as exhibits?

Yes, when the recipient permits exhibits. Label each exhibit clearly, refer to it in the affidavit, and ask whether the exhibit must also be presented during notarization.

Can I sign the affidavit before the online session?

Do not sign early when a jurat or witnessed signature is required. Follow the notary’s instructions so the correct act can be completed.

Does notarization prove that the affidavit is true?

No. The affiant is responsible for the truth of the statements. The notary verifies identity and completes the requested notarial act but does not investigate the facts.

Will every organization accept a general affidavit?

No. Some organizations require their own form or exact wording. Confirm the recipient’s requirements before creating, signing, or notarizing the affidavit.
