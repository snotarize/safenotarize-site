General Consent Letter

Use this guided template to give clear written consent for a specified activity or action. Review the completed document and confirm that it meets the receiving organization’s requirements before notarization.

Create This DocumentI Already Have the Completed PDF

Common uses

  • Providing a clear written statement or authorization.
  • Supporting a request from a school, business, agency, or service provider.
  • Creating a signed record that may require acknowledgment or an oath.

Before your notary session

  • Confirm the recipient accepts a general template.
  • Review names, dates, addresses, and identification details carefully.
  • Do not sign until you are connected with the notary unless instructed otherwise.

Who should use this document?

  • A person giving clear permission for a defined activity, decision, release, or limited action.
  • A school, service provider, caregiver, business, or other recipient that accepts a general written consent rather than a specialized form.

When is notarization commonly requested?

Schools, healthcare providers, financial institutions, businesses, travel providers, and government offices may request notarization to verify the consenting signer. The recipient may require a more specific consent, authorization, release, or official form.

Common mistakes to avoid

  • Using broad consent language without identifying the exact action.
  • Omitting the person or organization receiving permission.
  • Leaving dates, conditions, limits, or revocation terms unclear.
  • Using a general letter when a specialized consent form is required.

Compare these documents

These documents can appear in similar situations, but they serve different purposes. Confirm which one the receiving organization wants.

Consent Letter

Expresses permission or agreement to an action.

Authorization Letter

Allows another person to carry out a defined task.

Frequently asked questions

Can I limit the consent to one action or date range?

Yes. Clear limits, dates, conditions, and the receiving party’s name make the consent easier to understand and verify.

Can the receiving organization require its own form?

Yes. Schools, providers, agencies, and businesses commonly use specialized forms that may replace a general letter.

Important: This is a general template, not legal advice. Some organizations require their own form or specific wording.
